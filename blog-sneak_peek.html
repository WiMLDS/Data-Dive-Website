Hackathon Sneak Peek by Sinziana Eckner

Posted on November 2, 2015

We’ve all heard and read countless times how high healthcare spending in the United States is compared to other countries.

According to the Huffington Post, “among [this] group of big countries, the U.S. spends far and away more on healthcare than any other. And yet it has among the lowest life expectancies of any developed country”. The chart below drives the point home by showing healthcare expenditure versus life expectancy for OEDC countries.



But where do the inefficiencies lie? Can we unveil the sources by looking at some representative open datasets?

The way we chose the theme for our hackathon is to determine for ourselves what factors drive up healthcare costs and what their relative contributions are. Let’s give ourselves some wiggle room and acknowledge that we probably won’t be able to determine all the major cost drivers using the handful of datasets we chose, but we’d like to think we can get close to the meat of the problem.

This question is a bit intimidating... so let’s break it down into a few more digestible parts.

Healthcare is especially expensive for those suffering from chronic conditions. So how much are the sickest patients costing the system, and how do costs grow for a patients with multiple conditions? We will analyse the Chronic Conditions Public User Files provided by CMS to determine the contribution of chronic conditions versus one time conditions to Medicare and Medicaid costs.

There are an awful lot of medical malpractice law firms advertising their services in the NYC subway system - maybe a lucrative business? Let’s find out if this is the case via a malpractice claims dataset aggregated by enigma.io.

The Open Payments Data, which gives an insight into the economic dynamics between doctors, hospitals and manufacturers, was much publicised and disputed when it came out in 2013. According to the Wall Street Journal (see article) the published dollar amounts are not an accurate picture of reality. Regardless of whether we look at outliers or analyse the middle of the distribution, the Open Payments dataset should give us a good idea of the monetary exchanges impacting CMS.

These three datasets, along with the corresponding questions, are just a sneak peek of what we will be discussing, hacking, and investigating on the Saturday of November 7th. So let’s embark on a data adventure and find out where this quest will take us!
